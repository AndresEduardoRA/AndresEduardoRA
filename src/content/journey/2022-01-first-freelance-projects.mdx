## First Real Projects

After about a year of intensive learning, I'm feeling ready to build something for real users. I'm starting to take on small independent projects mostly through referrals from friends and family.

These aren't large commercial projects, but they're real:

- A small business website with a contact form
- An inventory management system for a local shop
- A simple booking system for a service provider

## New Challenges

Working on real projects is different from practice projects:

**Understanding requirements**: Clients often don't know exactly what they want. I have to ask the right questions.

**Managing expectations**: I'm learning to be clear about timelines and what's feasible.

**Handling feedback**: Real users have opinions and requests that I haven't anticipated.

**Responsibility**: If something doesn't work, someone's business is affected.

## What I'm Learning

**Communication matters**: Writing good code isn't enough. I need to explain technical decisions in simple terms.

**Simple solutions are often better**: I'm learning not to over-engineer. The goal is to solve the problem, not to use every technology I know.
I use AI assistants to draft clearer proposals and structure requirement lists so conversations with clients stay focused.

**Testing is crucial**: Bugs in production are much more stressful than bugs in practice projects.

## Balancing Studies and Projects

During this time, I'm still a full-time student. I have to balance:

- University coursework and exams
- Self-directed learning
- Independent projects
- Personal time (though I'm sacrificing a lot of social activities)

It's challenging, but it's teaching me time management and prioritization.

## Growth Focus

This period is helping me develop:

- Professional communication skills
- Project planning and estimation
- Writing maintainable, production-ready code
- Debugging and troubleshooting under pressure
